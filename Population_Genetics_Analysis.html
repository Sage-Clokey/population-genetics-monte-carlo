Discussion Questions & Answers

Q1: Why is the impact of genetic drift strongest in small, compared to large populations?

Genetic drift is the random change in allele frequency due to chance variation in which individuals reproduce in each generation. In small populations, each offspring represents a larger fraction of the total gene pool, so random sampling errors have a proportionally greater effect on allele frequencies.

Var(Δp) = p(1 − p) / 2N

This formula shows that the variance in allele frequency change per generation is inversely proportional to population size (N). As N decreases, variance increases — meaning larger random fluctuations occur each generation.

Evidence from simulations:
  • N=500, p=0.5: A1 was completely lost by generation ~2,000 — strong drift eliminated the allele in less than 20% of the total time span.
  • N=10,000, p=0.5: A1 fluctuated between 0.3–0.7 across the full 10,000 generations without ever reaching fixation or loss — drift was too weak to cause fixation.
  • N=500, p=0.1: A1 randomly surged from 0.1 to ~0.9 within ~1,000 generations, then crashed to 0 — a dramatic demonstration of large random swings possible in small populations.
Answer: Genetic drift is strongest in small populations because random sampling errors represent a larger proportion of the total gene pool each generation. With fewer individuals, a chance event (e.g., one allele not being passed on) has an outsized effect on overall allele frequency. In large populations, these random errors cancel out across thousands of individuals, producing minimal net change per generation.

Q2: Why do allele frequencies become uniform when population size is set to infinite?

Genetic drift is fundamentally a sampling phenomenon — it arises because a finite number of individuals reproduce each generation and alleles are randomly sampled. When population size is infinite, sampling error mathematically approaches zero.

Var(Δp) = p(1 − p) / 2N → 0 as N → ∞

With no variance in allele frequency change, allele frequencies cannot change from one generation to the next. This is the idealized condition described by the Hardy-Weinberg principle: in an infinitely large, randomly mating population with no evolutionary forces, allele and genotype frequencies remain constant indefinitely.

Evidence from simulations:
  • Infinind, p=0.5: Perfectly flat line at 0.5 for all 10,000 generations. Genotypes: A1A1=0.25, A1A2=0.50, A2A2=0.25 (exactly p² = 0.25, 2pq = 0.50, q² = 0.25).
  • Infinind, p=0.1: Perfectly flat line at 0.1. Genotypes: A1A1=0.01, A1A2=0.18, A2A2=0.81.
  • Infinind, p=0.9: Perfectly flat line at 0.9. Genotypes: A1A1=0.81, A1A2=0.18, A2A2=0.01.
  • In all three infinite cases, both allele and genotype frequency graphs were completely flat horizontal lines — no variation whatsoever across 10,000 generations.
Answer: With an infinite population size, every possible allele is transmitted in exact proportion each generation because there is no sampling error. Genetic drift, which requires a finite sample, cannot occur. Allele frequencies therefore remain perfectly stable ("uniform") across all generations, exactly matching their starting values and satisfying Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium.

Q3: When no evolution is occurring and genetic drift is minimal, what factor has the largest impact on the average final allele frequency of A1?

When drift is minimized (large or infinite N) and no evolutionary forces act (neutrality), the expected final allele frequency always equals the starting frequency. This is because drift is an unbiased process — it has no directional preference. On average across many replicate populations, the mean final frequency equals p₀ regardless of the number of generations.

Evidence from simulations (N=∞ and N=10,000):
Simulation Starting Freq (p₀) Approx. Final Freq
Infinind_0p1AF0.10.1 (unchanged)
Infinind_0p5AF0.50.5 (unchanged)
Infinind_0p9AF0.90.9 (unchanged)

Note: The N=10,000 p=0.1 result (loss) is consistent with the starting frequency — a rare allele (p=0.1) has only a 10% probability of fixation and a 90% probability of loss under neutrality, matching the expected outcome.

Answer: The starting allele frequency (p₀) has the largest impact on the average final allele frequency of A1. When drift is minimal and no evolutionary forces act, expected final frequency = p₀. The starting frequency also determines the probability of fixation under drift (probability of fixation = p₀), explaining why alleles starting at high frequency (p=0.9) are far more likely to become fixed than alleles starting at low frequency (p=0.1).

Summary

Variable Effect on A1 Allele Frequency
Small population size (N=500) Strong drift — rapid fixation or loss, large random fluctuations, outcomes within ~2k generations
Large population size (N=10,000) Weak drift — slow, small fluctuations; polymorphism maintained much longer
Infinite population size No drift — allele frequencies remain perfectly constant; Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium holds
Starting frequency (p₀ = 0.1) Low probability of fixation; allele likely to be lost to drift in finite populations
Starting frequency (p₀ = 0.5) Equal probability of fixation or loss; average final frequency ≈ 0.5
Starting frequency (p₀ = 0.9) High probability of fixation; allele likely to fix rapidly in small populations
